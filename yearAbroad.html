My Year Abroad at VU Amsterdam

In my third year of BSc Computer Science at the University of Leicester, I spent an academic year abroad at the Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam (VU Amsterdam) in the Netherlands. This exchange has been an incredible opportunity to explore new learning environments, collaborate internationally, and apply my technical and creative skills in unique ways.

Theories of Technology Venture Creation (TTV)

Theories of Technology Venture Creation explored how new and emerging technologies find their way to market within innovation ecosystems. The course examined the life cycle of high-tech companies through both theoretical frameworks and practical case studies, focusing on entrepreneurship, resource acquisition, and organizational innovation.

Key themes included understanding the implications of environment, business strategy, entrepreneurship, and resources for technology-driven companies. Grade: 6.0/10

Project Spotlight: Airshade Case Study Research

For our major group project, we conducted a comprehensive case study on AirShade, the world’s first air‑powered, fully passive shading system. Our research examined how this radically new technology succeeds in entering the highly conservative construction industry, focusing on the barriers the company faces and the strategies it uses to overcome them.

Our analysis explored three interconnected layers central to technology venture creation:

  • Environmental Barriers: Understanding how risk aversion, fragmented decision‑making, and new EU overheating regulations shape AirShade’s adoption challenges.
  • Legitimacy Building & Entrepreneurship: How the founder overcame the “liability of newness” through pilot projects, awards, storytelling, and collaborations with partners such as Arup, AMOLF, and Metadecor.
  • Resource Orchestration: How AirShade strategically leverages external engineering, testing facilities, and façade‑builder partnerships instead of owning factories or manufacturing equipment.
  • Value Proposition Design: How the product reduces installation complexity, eliminates electrical components, lowers maintenance costs, and appeals to both architects (design, sustainability) and contractors (risk reduction).

We based our research on a semi‑structured interview with the founder, supplemented with project reports, website data, and academic innovation theory. Through Gioia‑style qualitative analysis, we translated raw data into themes such as market conservatism, cognitive legitimacy, and partner‑enabled scalability.

This project strengthened my skills in qualitative research, theoretical modeling, and innovation strategy analysis. It also provided a clear view of how hardware startups must combine scientific validation, storytelling, and risk‑reducing design choices to achieve market adoption.

Our final report was well‑received for its clear conceptual model and practical explanation of how AirShade moves from experimental pilots to large architectural projects such as Masdar City, Hogeschool Utrecht, and the Venice Biennale pavilion.

Grade: 6.9/10

Project Spotlight: Risk‑Based Heuristic Agents for Schnapsen

For this course, our group developed three intelligent Schnapsen‑playing agents based on risk‑aware decision‑making: an opponent‑reactive bot, a risk‑control bot, and a score‑based adaptive bot. We evaluated them against a static machine‑learning baseline to study how different risk‑management heuristics influence performance in an imperfect‑information game.

Our research introduced and analysed three dynamic heuristic strategies:

  • Opponent‑Reactive Bot: adapts to perceived opponent aggression, adjusting strategy across game phases and exploiting behavioural cues.
  • Risk‑Control Bot: preserves high‑value cards, avoids risky plays, and focuses on stable, low‑variance decision‑making.
  • Score‑Based Bot: alternates between aggressive and conservative play depending on whether it is currently winning or losing.

Using 300,000 tournament games, we found that:

  • The Opponent‑Reactive Bot achieved the highest win rate overall.
  • The Risk‑Control Bot consistently outperformed the ML bot through stability and low‑risk heuristics.
  • The Score‑Based Bot underperformed due to its narrow focus on score differential.

This project strengthened my expertise in AI agent design, heuristic reasoning, experimental evaluation, and scientific reporting. Our final academic paper, “Risk‑Based Dynamic Heuristics for Intelligent Agents Playing Schnapsen”, details our methodology, agent implementations, and comparative results.

Note: The Report has no author mentioned as it was required by the professors for grading purposes.

Courses in Period 4

Computational Intelligence

Computational Intelligence focuses on the algorithmic foundations of Artificial Intelligence, with an emphasis on solving complex optimization and learning problems. The course explores both derivative-free methods and gradient-based techniques, alongside modern AI approaches such as deep learning, reinforcement learning, and neuroevolution.

Core topics include hill climbing, local search, evolutionary algorithms, Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods, neural networks (including convolutional architectures), and Q-learning. The course develops a strong understanding of how to select, analyse, and apply optimization techniques to real-world AI problems.

Through a combination of theoretical learning and practical assignments, I developed the ability to:

  • apply optimization algorithms to complex, non-differentiable problems
  • design and analyse evolutionary algorithms for specific use cases
  • select appropriate neural network architectures for different tasks
  • understand reinforcement learning fundamentals such as Q-learning
  • evaluate algorithm performance and justify methodological choices

Project Spotlight: Analysing Spotify Data

As part of this course, our group developed a data-driven dashboard using a large-scale Spotify dataset (2023), containing information on artists, albums, tracks, and audio features. The goal was to transform raw data into meaningful insights for business analysts.

The project involved multiple stages, including:

  • Exploratory Data Analysis: analysing artist popularity, followers, and genre distributions
  • Statistical Modelling: investigating relationships such as popularity vs followers using regression
  • Database Integration: querying relational datasets using SQL (SQLite)
  • Data Wrangling: handling missing data, duplicates, and feature engineering
  • Feature Analysis: studying trends across time, genres, and musical attributes

We developed an interactive Streamlit dashboard that allows users to:

  • explore artist and genre statistics dynamically
  • analyse trends across different time periods
  • search and compare artists based on popularity and features
  • visualise key insights through interactive graphs

The project was managed using GitHub with proper version control practices, and deployed online for public access. This experience strengthened my skills in data engineering, collaborative development, and building production-ready data applications.
